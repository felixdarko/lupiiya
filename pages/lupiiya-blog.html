Uganda's crowdfunding culture

One of the things that makes me proud to be a Ugandan is the strength of our communities. We have some of the strongest community setups where every individual has an immediate network of at least a dozen close friends and family members that they can rely on when in need. I have attended funerals in Uganda that happen a day or two after the deceased has passed that have been attended by hundreds, if not thousands of people that all had to travel long distances with very little notice. This is resounding evidence of the strength of our communities.

Can Africa enterprise in a knowledge-based global economy?

In my last blog post, I questioned and indeed critiqued the notion that entrepreneurship is to be the vehicle with which Africa solves her most immediate issues. Issues ranging from poverty, high unemployment, a majority youth populous, majority of whom are idle. While that blog was poignant, it barely began to scratch the surface of how ill-suited for large scale entrepreneurship the current African setup is. In this article, I am going to continue to expound on what I believe to be one of the main hindrances to Africa's 'development by entrepreneurship' goal.

Is entrepreneurship really the silver bullet to Africa’s youth unemployment problem?

According to a report by the African Development Bank, there are over 200 million young Africans between the ages of 15 and 24, by far the most on any continent. Furthermore, the report goes on to state that 53 million of them are in unstable employment situations, very often linked to working poverty. 40 million young Africans are out of work. Only 18 million of them are looking for a job, however, 22 million have given up looking for one.
